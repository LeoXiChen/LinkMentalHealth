How culture and religion impacted the stigma surrounding mental health for me

Mariam's Story

For some context, I have struggled with mental health issues for most of my life. I started showing signs of anxiety and depression when I was around 10 years old when I started isolating myself from people. At 14, I was diagnosed with clinical depression and anxiety and when I was 22, my diagnosis changed to bipolar and general anxiety. My first diagnosis came after I suffered a great loss of someone in my life and that really triggered my depressive episodes. Coming from a family where half of them are Egyptian and Muslim, I felt it was taboo for me to discuss my issues. Every time I tried to explain how I was a feeling and I was just being “disrespectful” I was just truly sad, my family would just tell me to keep praying, read Quran and move on with life. It is really problematic when communities that are predominately Muslim say there is no mental illness in our religion. Our beloved Prophet Muhammed (Peace and Blessings be upon him) suffered from great sadness and he was and is the greatest man to have ever lived in our eyes.

My depression got better but when I was 21 almost 22 years old, I suffered a severe mental collapse and was hospitalized for 3 weeks in the mental health ward. That experience has made me grow distant to some of the closest people to me biologically. I have had to hide the fact on why my bachelor’s degree is taking me 7 years to finish because of the mental health issues I have endured.

I am very blessed to have an understanding husband, mother and step-mother who have helped me navigate through my mental health issues but I really hope some unnamed people would understand and be there for me more. It broke my heart in the beginning when I had to cut ties with some people but I truly would love to reconnect on the basis that they start to understand how complex my issues are.

That is one hope. My other hope is that I grow with my issues and learn how to navigate through them; to use tools like therapy as a proactive way of thinking not a reactive way of thinking. I am almost there and I have truly come along way since my hospitalization in March 2017.
