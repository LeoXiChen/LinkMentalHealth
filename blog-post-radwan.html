Therapy - The Fishing Rod I Just Couldn't Find

Radwan's Story

“People love to say, “Give someone a fish, and they’ll eat for a day. Teach someone to fish, and they’ll eat for a lifetime.”

What they don’t say is, “And it’d be nice if you gave them a fishing rod.” That’s the part of the analogy that’s missing.

I recently heard that quote from Trevor Noah and could not help but draw the parallel to the mental health system that exists in in our country.

Two years ago, I was told by the counseling centre at my university that I should seek on-going therapy. In a weird twist of fate, I actually felt relief hearing there was something that could explain why I felt the way I did, and that there was a proven way to help me manage it all.

I found my fish and knew that if I just had a fishing rod, I would be alright.

It was pulled away from me even before I had my hands on it, as I was told that I would be put on a 6 month wait list. Worst yet, I later learned that I had health coverage through my student union during this period, something I wasn’t aware of throughout my experience as a student. So began an extremely difficult 6 month period. If I wasn’t already a horrible swimmer, I’d say something like “even swimming became difficult, nevermind fishing.”

Looking back, I probably gave very few signs that I was going through something to most people I interacted with. I was very involved in the university community, even running several campaigns that were lucky enough to gain media coverage. To many except those closest, there likely were no signs that I needed to learn to cope, much less have to do it fast. It wasn’t until later, when I COMPLETELY pulled myself away from everything that used to interest me that some began to notice.

That didn’t just happen overnight. Nevertheless, I would wait my time and go through the therapy needed to give me the tools that would help me complete my degree.

Fast forward to a year later, recently graduated, I would recognize a hard truth: I would not be able to seek the services at the university any more. Who would have thought trying to find help after graduating would be even tougher than a 6 month waitlist? And if you’re not surprised to read that- why the hell should that be accepted as status quo?

I can confidently say that it should not be acceptable to the 4.5 million Canadians who aren’t getting the help they need today. Or the communities that only feel comfortable speaking with someone who can relate to their ethnic background. Or that only 13% of Canadians know about their health insurance very well. Think about that for a moment- those with access to coverage still feel like mental health is inaccessible. I can’t begin to explain how paralyzing that feels.

Nah. That isn’t going to cut it. Finding help should be easy, whether for you or for someone you know. Now more understanding of the deep flaws in the system than ever and in a better state, I knew I needed to do something about it.

After much work, I’m excited to write that my team at LinkMe has a way to make that possible. In the process of making it happen, I discovered resources I did not know were accessible when I needed them, including free therapy programs with far shorter wait-times than what we often hear about. We have integrated those resources and combined the public, private and non-profit services that exist in a really easy to navigate platform. www.linkme.care is a website that connects people to mental health services that best fits what they’re looking for, regardless of what they can afford. It’s time we stop telling people to “get help” and give them that dang fishing rod.
